    Why the Church Should Take Responsibility for Its Liberties and Freedoms

    March 12, 2024 · 3 min
    Table of Contents

    • Free Speech and Press Freedoms

    The role of the church in a democratic society is often debated. Some see the church as solely a spiritual place, advocating for moral or biblical principles in the public sphere. Others argue the church should remain separate from politics, and limited to preaching and religious worship. However, Christian influence on public freedoms and liberties is also significant, albeit not as obvious. Therefore, the church should take responsibility for its part in shaping and preserving liberties and freedoms that underpin a well-functioning democracy.

    Religious Freedom and the Church's Impact

    The concept of religious liberty is central to democratic societies and has its roots in religious principles. The Church has historically advanced this principle, advocating for believers to freely worship God without fear of persecution or punishment. The First Amendment of the US Constitution, for instance, includes a guarantee of religious freedom, a legacy of early Christian advocacy.

    The Magna Carta, English Bill of Rights, and various European treaties that protect religious liberties also have roots in religious thought, particularly as it relates to the protection of individual conscience.

    Today, the church still plays a vital role in ensuring religious freedom is upheld and fostering a peaceful coexistence among people of different faiths. The church serves as a platform to educate about religious tolerance, promote interfaith dialogue, and fight against religious discrimination. It has a moral imperative to continue advocating for these values, as they are often at the core of Christian ethos.

    Free Speech and Press Freedoms

    Religious roots have also influenced free speech and press freedoms. The Greeks and Romans granted some freedom of expression, but Christians further developed this idea. During the Reformation, Martin Luther and John Wycliffe advocated for the right to disseminate their ideas and beliefs through printed works, in spite of the dangers and persecution. This emphasis on the right to share one’s opinions birthed elements of modern free speech protections.

    Today, the church can continue to uphold the importance of the free exchange of ideas in a democratic society. Leaders can model civil discourse, call for an environment where diverse viewpoints are heard, and advocate for truth and objectivity in news reporting.

    The Pursuit of Life, Liberty, and Happiness

    In the US, the Declaration of Independence’s assertion that "all men are created equal, endowed by their Creator with certain unalienable rights" underscores the religious underpinning of human rights. The Church, in its teachings, has historically upheld the inherent dignity and worth of every individual. This has informed and shaped the framework of human rights in modern democracies.

    Through advocacy for fair labor practices, humanitarian assistance, and aid to the poor, the church continues to protect and advance personal liberties. In standing up for the oppressed, the marginalized, and the voiceless, the church can draw attention to societal injustices and be a catalyst for change.

    Conclusion

    The Church has significantly contributed to the development and maintenance of public liberties and freedoms. Though separate from political institutions, it has the moral and ethical responsibility to uphold these values. Through fostering religious tolerance, protecting religious freedom, supporting free speech and press, and championing human rights, the church demonstrates the very foundational principles that nurture a healthy democracy.
